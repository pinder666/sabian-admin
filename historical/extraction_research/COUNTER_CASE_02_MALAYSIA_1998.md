# COUNTER-CASE 02 — MALAYSIA 1998
## "Capital Controls as a Weapon — The Unconventional Escape"

---

## The Setup

Malaysia in 1997-1998 faced the same pressures as Thailand, Indonesia, and Korea:
- Ringgit under speculative attack
- Capital flight accelerating
- Stock market collapsed 75% from peak
- Banking system stressed

Every IMF orthodoxy said: accept the program, float the currency, open the capital account.

Malaysia did the opposite. And it worked.

## What Malaysia Did Differently

### 1. Rejected the IMF
Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad refused an IMF program in 1997-1998. He publicly accused currency speculators (Soros specifically) of destroying Asian economies. He was condemned by international financial institutions.

He was also right.

### 2. Imposed Capital Controls — September 1998
On September 1, 1998, Malaysia:
- Pegged the ringgit at 3.80 per USD
- Prohibited offshore trading of ringgit
- Restricted repatriation of portfolio investment for 12 months
- Required shareholder approval for significant asset transfers

This was the opposite of what every economist recommended. The IMF predicted it would worsen the crisis.

### 3. Locked Out the Speculators
The capital controls eliminated the mechanism speculators use: borrow ringgit, sell for USD, profit when ringgit falls. With offshore trading prohibited, there was no mechanism to short the currency. The attack ended.

### 4. Bought Back Assets at Low Prices Domestically
With capital controls in place and the ringgit pegged, Malaysian entities (government-linked companies, domestic institutions) were able to acquire distressed assets at low prices — before foreign vultures could enter. The asset capture went domestic rather than foreign.

### 5. Recovered Without Restructuring the Financial System
Unlike Korea (which forced chaebol restructuring) and Indonesia (which closed 16 banks), Malaysia maintained its banking system largely intact. The capital controls bought time to recapitalize rather than liquidate.

---

## Why Extractors Did Not Succeed

### Capital Controls Blocked the Entry Mechanism
The currency attack playbook requires free capital movement. You borrow local currency, sell for hard currency, wait for devaluation, repay in devalued currency. Capital controls broke this chain.

### No IMF Conditionality = No Bank Closures = No Bank Runs
Indonesia's 1997 crisis was worsened when IMF conditions required closing 16 banks, triggering bank runs on surviving institutions. Malaysia's refusal of the IMF meant no such shock was delivered.

### No Bond Default
Malaysia maintained all external obligations. Without default, no distressed bond buying. Without distressed bonds, no vulture litigation.

### Asset Recovery Kept Domestic
The government-linked investment vehicle Danaharta acquired non-performing loans from banks at a discount — keeping asset recovery within domestic control rather than allowing foreign distressed buyers to acquire Malaysian bank assets.

## The Unconventional Signature

This is the counter-case that breaks the rule. Everything Sabian detects — capital flow reversal, economic stress, currency pressure — was present in Malaysia. The escape did not come from fixing the fundamentals. It came from blocking the mechanism of extraction.

| Element | Malaysia 1998 | Extraction Pattern |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| IMF program | Rejected | Accepted with conditionality |
| Capital controls | Imposed | Opposed by IMF |
| Currency | Pegged (locked) | Floated (devalued) |
| Asset recovery | Domestic (Danaharta) | Foreign buyers |
| Default | No | Required for vulture entry |
| Recovery speed | ~18 months | 5+ years for crisis cases |

---

## The Controversy

Malaysia's escape is the most contested counter-case in international finance. The IMF and most Western economists argued the controls would cause long-term capital flight and structural damage. They were wrong in the short run. Whether they were right in the long run (Malaysia growing slower than potential) is still debated.

**The lesson for Sabian**: The capital controls signal is detectable. When a government imposes capital controls, it is simultaneously:
1. An indicator of desperation (crisis is severe enough to require drastic action)
2. A potential escape mechanism (blocking the extraction entry point)

The same event is both a crisis signal and a potential escape signal depending on timing and execution.
