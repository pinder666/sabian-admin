# COUNTER-CASE 05 — JAMAICA 2010 / 2013
## "Domestic Debt Restructuring — Removing Jurisdictional Access"

---

## The Setup

Jamaica in 2010-2013 was one of the most indebted countries in the world:
- Debt-to-GDP: 140% (among the highest in the Western Hemisphere)
- Growth: near-zero for a decade
- Two IMF programs
- Two debt restructurings in three years

This should have been a perfect extraction scenario. It was not.

## What Jamaica Did Differently

### 1. Domestic Debt, Not International Bonds
Jamaica's debt was predominantly domestic — held by Jamaican banks, pension funds, insurance companies, and domestic investors. This is the structural fact that made the escape possible.

Elliott Management, Aurelius, Dart — these entities operate in international bond markets under New York or English law. They cannot file a pari passu claim in a Jamaican court against a Jamaican government for Jamaican-law debt held domestically.

Jurisdiction matters. The extraction playbook requires access to international courts.

### 2. Jamaica Debt Exchange (JDX) — February 2010
The JDX was a voluntary exchange offering lower interest rates over longer maturities. 99% of eligible holders participated.

The 99% participation rate means:
- No meaningful holdout population
- No distressed trading opportunity (bonds not at 10-30 cents)
- No SDNY litigation possible (domestic bonds, domestic courts)

The government achieved this by working directly with domestic financial institutions before announcement, building consensus rather than springing a surprise.

### 3. National Debt Exchange (NDX2) — February 2013
Three years later, the same approach: voluntary exchange, domestic debt, domestic holders, domestic courts. IMF program alongside (Extended Fund Facility, $932M).

Again, near-100% participation. Again, no vulture play.

### 4. IMF Program With Different Framing
The 2013 IMF program was designed around what Jamaica could actually deliver, not IMF textbook conditionality. The IMF had learned from Indonesia 1997. The program included fiscal consolidation but avoided shock-therapy bank closures that trigger panic.

---

## Why Extractors Did Not Succeed

### Jurisdictional Wall
The single most important factor: the debt was domestic, under Jamaican law, held by Jamaican institutions. International vulture funds have no mechanism to purchase this debt (not publicly traded on international exchanges) and no court to file in (not subject to SDNY or UK jurisdiction).

### 99% Participation Leaves No Holdout Opportunity
Even if a vulture fund somehow acquired Jamaican domestic bonds, 99% participation in the exchange leaves no pari passu leverage. The remaining 1% cannot block payments to the 99%.

### Speed and Relationship Management
Both restructurings were completed in weeks because the government pre-negotiated with the actual holders (domestic pension funds and banks) before public announcement. By the time any international entity understood what was happening, it was done.

### No External Bond Default
Jamaica maintained all external (international bond market) obligations. This preserved its ability to re-access international capital markets post-restructuring and eliminated the distressed external bond buying opportunity.

## The Domestic Debt Insight

Jamaica is the clearest proof that the type of debt matters as much as the amount.

140% debt-to-GDP in domestic currency, under domestic law, held by domestic institutions = manageable through domestic negotiation.

140% debt-to-GDP in external currency, under New York law, held by international funds = extraction target.

**For Sabian**: The composition of a country's debt — domestic vs. external, currency denomination, governing law — is a structural factor that determines how vulnerable the country is to the extraction patterns. Two countries with identical economic stress signals may have vastly different extraction risk based solely on their debt composition.

This is a data layer Sabian does not currently capture. IMF Article IV reports contain this information. World Bank International Debt Statistics contain it. This is worth adding to the signal set as a structural overlay.

## The Two-Restructuring Pattern

Jamaica is one of the few countries to execute two clean restructurings within three years with no extraction. The pattern:

1. First restructuring (JDX 2010): Buys time, reduces debt service
2. Fiscal adjustment period: Government actually delivers primary surplus
3. Second restructuring (NDX2 2013): Refinances remaining burden
4. IMF exit: Jamaica exited the IMF program in 2019 and graduated to precautionary status

The two-restructuring pattern without extraction is historically rare. It required:
- Domestic debt composition
- Near-unanimous participation each time
- Genuine fiscal adjustment (Jamaica ran a primary surplus of 7%+ of GDP)
- Political durability across two different governments (JLP and PNP)
