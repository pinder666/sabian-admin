# COUNTER-CASE 03 — URUGUAY 2003
## "Collective Action Clauses as Extraction Defense"

---

## The Setup

Uruguay 2002-2003 looked like the beginning of Argentina 2001 all over again:
- Argentina contagion spreading across the Río de la Plata
- Bank run in February 2002 wiped out $800M in deposits in a single week
- GDP collapsed 11% in 2002
- Uruguay requested emergency IMF support
- Sovereign debt restructuring required

Argentina's default produced the most profitable vulture fund play in history: Elliott/NML made 1,270% over 15 years.

Uruguay's restructuring in 2003 produced nothing for vulture funds.

## What Uruguay Did Differently

### 1. Used Collective Action Clauses (CACs)
Uruguay's bond issuances — particularly New York-law bonds — contained Collective Action Clauses. CACs allow a supermajority of bondholders to bind all holders to a restructuring agreement, eliminating the holdout strategy.

When Uruguay restructured in 2003, the CACs activated. 93% participation triggered the clauses. The 7% that wanted to hold out were legally bound by the majority vote.

Elliott Management's entire Argentina strategy depended on the absence of CACs in Argentine bonds issued under New York law. Those pre-CAC bonds gave each holder a veto. Uruguay's bonds did not.

### 2. Voluntary Exchange, Not Default
Uruguay did not default. It conducted a voluntary exchange: extend maturities by 3 years, reduce interest rates, restructure on terms bondholders could accept. This is technically a restructuring but not a payment default under ISDA definitions.

Without a technical default, there is no distressed debt to buy at 10-30 cents. Without distressed debt, there is no vulture litigation. The voluntary structure eliminated the entry point.

### 3. Fast and Complete
The restructuring was announced, executed, and completed in under 3 months. Speed prevented accumulation. By the time distressed funds identified Uruguay as a target, 93% participation was already locked.

### 4. IMF Supported but Did Not Control
The IMF provided bridge financing ($3.8B) but was not setting conditionality that could trigger social unrest or bank panics. The program was structured to support the voluntary exchange, not impose structural adjustment.

### 5. Maintained Banking System
Unlike Argentina (which froze deposits — the "corralito"), Uruguay allowed deposit withdrawals but the IMF bridge financing backstopped the system. The bank run was contained, not amplified.

---

## Why Extractors Did Not Succeed

### CACs Eliminated the Holdout Strategy
This is the single most important factor. The pari passu doctrine — that all bondholders have equal treatment — creates leverage for holdouts only when each holdout has individual veto power. CACs removed that veto.

### Voluntary Structure Means No Distressed Entry Price
Default drops bond prices to 10-30 cents, creating the buying opportunity. A voluntary exchange maintains bond prices closer to restructured value (60-80 cents). There is no 1,270% return available when you buy at 70 cents.

### Speed Compressed the Window
The 3-month timeline from announcement to completion was too short for systematic accumulation. Elliott spent years accumulating Argentine debt. Three months is not enough time to identify, acquire, and position a litigation strategy.

---

## The CAC Lesson

Uruguay 2003 is cited in every sovereign debt restructuring paper written after 2003 as the proof that CACs work. After Uruguay, the IMF and US Treasury began pushing for universal CAC inclusion in sovereign bonds. By 2014, most new sovereign bonds (including under the EU Collective Action framework) include CACs.

This means the Elliott-style holdout strategy is becoming structurally blocked for new issuances. But legacy bonds (pre-2003, especially Argentine bonds) remain vulnerable.

**For Sabian**: The presence or absence of CACs in a country's bond documentation is a detectable factor. Countries with old pre-CAC bonds in distress are more vulnerable to the vulture play than countries with CAC-protected issuances.
